This condensed version of the Monetary Policy Report (MPR)
is designed to make monetary policy easier to understand
for the public. Our goal is to help people better grasp and act
on the BSP's views and actions, particularly on inflation and
interest rates.

To do this, we have shortened the content. While some
technical information has been omitted, we hope this version
will provide a clearer picture of monetary policymaking.

Foreword

(From the official Monetary Policy Report)

The primary objective of monetary policy is to promote low and stable
inflation conducive to balanced and sustainable economic growth
and employment. To help fulfill this objective, the Bangko Sentral ng
Pilipinas (BSP) adopted the inflation targeting framework for monetary
policy.

One of the key features of inflation targeting is greater transparency.
This means greater disclosure and communication by the BSP of
its policy actions and decisions. The Monetary Policy Report plays
a primary part in the BSP’s effort at transparency. It shares with
stakeholders, including market participants, the latest assessment
of the monetary policy stance based on an analysis of economic and
financial prospects. The broad aim is to make the conduct of monetary
policy more accessible to the public. Through the Monetary Policy
Report, the public may better understand and monitor the BSP’s
commitment to the inflation target.

The Monetary Policy Report now shows a central forecast or projection
representing the BSP's view of the most likely path of inflation
and domestic economic activity. Alternative quantitative scenarios
complement these forecasts to illustrate the potential impact of risks
to the outlook. These alternative scenarios provide a more consistent
approach to communicating inflation risks and uncertainty compared
with fan charts. Further details on the BSP’s central forecasts can be
found in Box Article 1 (Understanding the BSP’s Central Projections and
Alternative Scenarios).

Under the inflation targeting framework, the Development Budget
Coordination Committee (DBCC) has retained the government’s annual
headline inflation target at 3.0 percent ± 1.0 percentage point (ppt) for
2025–2028. The inflation target range continues to be our quantitative
representation of the medium-term goal of price stability, aligned with
the current structure of the Philippine economy and the outlook on
macroeconomic conditions over the next few years.

The Monetary Board approved this Monetary Policy Report at its
meeting on 19 June 2025.

The BSP mandate

The main responsibility of the BSP is to formulate and implement policy in the
areas of money, banking, and credit, with the primary objective of maintaining
stable prices conducive to balanced and sustainable economic growth and
employment in the Philippines. The BSP also aims to promote and preserve
monetary stability and the convertibility of the national currency.

Monetary policy instruments

The BSP implements monetary policy by setting the target reverse repurchase
(RRP) rate. It conducts liquidity management operations through the overnight
RRP facility, term deposit facility (TDF), and BSP Securities Facility (BSP-SF) to
ensure that the overnight RRP rate is close to the target RRP rate.

Policy target

The BSP’s target for monetary policy is the headline inflation, which is the
average annual inflation rate based on the Consumer Price Index (CPI) compiled
and released to the public by the Philippine Statistics Authority (PSA). The policy
target is set by the DBCC in consultation with the BSP.1 The inflation target for
2024–2028 is 3.0 percent ± 1.0 ppt.1

Interest rate adjustment

On 19 June 2025, the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP)
decided to reduce its key monetary policy interest rate,
the target reverse repurchase (RRP) rate, by 25 basis
points (bps) to 5.25 percent.

The BSP also reduced its overnight deposit and lending
rates for banks to 4.75 percent and 5.75 percent,
respectively.

Reason behind the decision

The decision reflects a more moderate inflation outlook
for 2025 and a slightly higher inflation path for 2026 and
2027. The inflation forecast for 2025 fell from 2.4 percent
to 1.6 percent. Meanwhile, forecasts for 2026 increased
slightly from 3.3 percent to 3.4 percent and from
3.2 percent to 3.3 percent for 2027.

Signs of global economic slowdown, driven by
uncertainty surrounding US trade policy and the
conflict in the Middle East, could lead to slower
domestic growth.

The slower-than-expected economic growth and
global uncertainty prompted the need for a more
accommodative stance.

Inflation forecast

Inflation forecasts

According to the BSP’s latest estimates, inflation is expected to remain below the
2.0 to 4.0 target range through Q4 2025 and within the range in 2026 and 2027.
The central projection2  shows average inflation at 1.6 percent in 2025, 3.4 percent
in 2026, and 3.3 percent in 2027.

Over the near term, risks to the inflation outlook remain broadly balanced. Both
high- and low-inflation scenarios suggest inflation will be below target in 2025.
Meanwhile, inflation is seen to remain within the target range under the low-
inflation scenario for 2026 and 2027.

The high-inflation scenario suggests that inflation could exceed the target due
to potential supply-side pressures. These potential pressures include increases
in electricity rates, higher oil prices, and a possible return to higher rice tariffs.
Conversely, the low-inflation scenario accounts for the possible impact of US
policies on global trade and investment, which could dampen domestic economic
activity.

Meanwhile, results from the BSP’s June 2025 survey of external forecasters (BSEF)
indicated within-target inflation expectations from 2025 to 2027. Upside risks
include geopolitical tensions, trade disruptions, commodity supply shocks, higher
import costs, and possible increases in electricity rates. Downside risks include
lower rice prices and weaker domestic and global growth.

External factors

Global economic outlook

The May 2025 Global Projection Model Network forecasts indicate
that the global economy is expected to decelerate, with the global
output gap projected to turn negative in Q2 2025. Similarly, the April
2025 World Economic Outlook (WEO) update from the International
Monetary Fund (IMF) suggests a similar trend, factoring in the 90-day
pause in the implementation of higher US tariffs and the renegotiation
of bilateral trade terms with the US.

Global crude oil prices

Domestic oil prices are expected to follow the assumed lower prices of
international crude oil. Dubai crude oil prices have fallen since February
2025 but have risen anew due to concerns over the Iran-Israel conflict.
However, this will be balanced by lower global oil demand following the
implementation of new US tariffs.

World non-oil prices

The IMF’s WEO update indicated that non-fuel commodity prices
are expected to rise this year. Prices of precious metals, such as gold,
have increased due to strong demand for safe haven assets amid
geopolitical tensions and uncertainty. Base metals prices faced
demand pressures from front-loading ahead of anticipated tariffs but
declined in the first week of April as trade tensions escalated.

Recent Developments

₱

Price conditions.  Headline inflation settled at 1.3 percent in
May, below the 2.0-4.0 percent target. Lower electricity rates and
domestic petroleum prices helped reduce non-food inflation.
Upticks in food prices were offset by slower inflation for key non-
food items.

Food inflation stayed at 0.7 percent. Rice prices continue to
decrease due to lower international prices, ample supply from the
ongoing dry season harvest, and direct government interventions.

Demand conditions. Output expanded by 5.4 percent in Q1 2025,
below the government’s 2025 growth target range of 6.0–8.0
percent. Nevertheless, the country recorded the second-fastest
growth among Asian economies for the period. Vietnam led with
6.9-percent growth, while China joined the Philippines at 5.4
percent. Indonesia grew by 4.9 percent, Malaysia by 4.4 percent,
and Singapore by 3.8 percent.

Household consumption accelerated, driven by increased
spending on food and non-alcoholic beverages, transport, and
restaurant and hotel services. Government expenditure also
increased, reflecting higher maintenance and operating expenses
(Figure 2).

Labor conditions. The unemployment rate slightly rose to 4.1
percent in April as labor force growth exceeded employment
gains. The underemployment rate stayed at 14.6 percent,
although the number of underemployed individuals rose
modestly to 7.1 million from 7.0 million a year earlier.

Monetary operations.  As of June 2025, the BSP absorbed
₱1.6 trillion in liquidity. The BSP Securities Facility accounted
for 44.0 percent of total placements. Term Deposit Facilities
(TDF) represented 12.0 percent, while the overnight reverse
repurchase (ON RRP) and overnight deposit facilities (ODF)
comprised 28.5 percent and 15.4 percent, respectively. These
operations effectively kept the ON RRP rate aligned with the
target RRP rate.

Financial conditions. Domestic liquidity (M3) grew at a slower
pace, fueled by sustained bank lending to non-financial private
corporations and households., alongside investments in
government securities.

Outstanding loans from universal and commercial banks (net
of reverse repurchase placements) grew by 11.2 percent in April,
slightly below the 11.8 percent growth in March.

The moderation largely reflected slower expansion in lending
to real estate, wholesale and retail trade, manufacturing,
financial and insurance activities, information and
communication, and transportation sectors. In contrast,
consumer loans accelerated, mainly driven by credit card
lending.

Capital market. The primary government securities (GS)
market in June 2025 reflected expectations of further
slowdown in inflation. The Bureau of the Treasury (BTr) raised
₱28.6 billion through Treasury bill (T-bill) auctions, exceeding
the initial ₱25.0-billion offering.

Secondary market GS yields were mixed as participants
weighed the prospects for further BSP easing amid outlook of
below-target inflation for this year, and expected acceleration
to within-target in the next two years. As of 4 June 2025, the
spreads between the 10-year and 1-year GS rates widened to
60.9 bps (from 51.5 bps at end-April), and the spread between
the 10-year and 5-year rates widened to 40.0 bps (from 35.6
bps). These shifts stemmed from a rise in the 10-year rate,
alongside a decrease in the 1-year rate and a marginal increase
in the 5-year rate.

The peso appreciated in May, driven by a weaker US dollar, a 5-year low in domestic
inflation, higher overseas Filipino remittances, and a narrowing trade deficit. These
gains were partially offset by below-target GDP growth, lower gross international
reserves (GIR), weaker foreign direct investment (FDI), and a higher balance of
payments deficit. Year-to-date, the peso closed at ₱55.77/US$1 as of 4 June 2025,
appreciating by 3.72 percent relative to end-December 2024.

External developments. Global economic activity remained
subdued, with divergent performance across sectors and regions.
In May, the JP Morgan All-Industry Output Index showed a slight
increase from April’s low. However, momentum remained weak
due to a sharp slowdown in activity in emerging markets, especially
China, combined with weak manufacturing activity and trade-
related concerns. Despite some positive signs from the US and the
eurozone, overall prospects stayed muted amid trade tensions.9

The IMF’s April 2025 WEO update cut the global growth forecast to 2.8 percent.
This reflects (a) resurgent trade protectionism that is disrupting supply chains and
dampening investment; (b) heightened policy uncertainty amid uncoordinated fiscal
and trade measures in major economies; and (c) persistent inflationary pressures
complicating monetary policy amid uneven demand-supply dynamics.

Advanced economies, including the US, face tighter financial conditions and slower
consumption. Emerging markets and developing economies confront additional
headwinds from escalating trade tensions and restrictive measures, with China’s
slowdown affecting the region.

Policy actions. Most central banks monitored by the BSP
maintained a cautious tone in their monetary policy stance amid
elevated uncertainty. Some central banks are ready to pursue
further rate cuts as economic conditions evolve.

In their May 2025 meetings, the Bank of England, People’s Bank of
China, Reserve Bank of Australia, Bank Indonesia, Reserve Bank of

New Zealand, and Bank of Korea cut policy rates. Meanwhile, the Bank of Canada
kept its policy rate unchanged.
